A Week of Printing, 100+ Magnets, and One Very Unique Map

Nathan Walker  ·  February 27, 2026  ·  6 min read

Sometimes the best projects start with a simple thought: What if you could hold a GIS map in your hands? Not just a flat map, but a fully interactive 3D model that shows how political boundaries cut across real terrain.

That idea turned into the 3D Wisconsin Puzzle Elevation Map and somehow ended up winning Best in Category – Most Unique at WLIA's annual Map Contest.

The concept was simple: each county in Wisconsin becomes its own 3D-printed terrain tile. When assembled, they form the entire state. But instead of gluing the pieces together, I embedded precisely sized magnets into the bottom of each piece so the map can be pushed together to form the whole state on a fridge or other magnetic surface.

No glue. No fasteners. Just magnets on the bottom so WLIA attendees could build the state they manage so well.

The finished model took over a week of continuous print time, used more than 3,000 grams of filament, and required over 100 embedded magnets. If you include coding, testing print settings, experimenting with smoothing techniques, and making sure every county fit perfectly with its neighbors, the total time easily surpassed 100 hours.

Honestly, figuring it all out was half the fun.

Turning GIS Data into Physical Terrain

One of the biggest technical challenges was making sure each county model aligned perfectly with the ones around it.

Plenty of tools can convert a digital elevation model (DEM) into a 3D surface. Packages like rayshader in R can do beautiful terrain rendering. But they generally assume square raster extents, which doesn't work when you're trying to make puzzle pieces shaped like counties.

So I took a different approach.

I wrote a custom Python workflow that loops through every county in Wisconsin and masks a DEM provided by the Wisconsin DNR. Once each county was isolated, I had to convert that masked elevation data into a printable 3D model.

Since no existing package handled that exact workflow, I built the conversion myself using:

  • rasterio for working with the elevation data
  • shapely for geometry operations
  • trimesh for constructing the final 3D meshes

The first generation of models turned out very detailed — and much too detailed for printing. The terrain looked noisy and exaggerated at the scale of the map.

To fix that, I applied Gaussian smoothing using SciPy, experimenting with different values for the sigma parameter until the terrain looked more natural and readable. After a few iterations, the models finally had the right balance between detail and printability.

The Result

The final map lets you do something that's hard to appreciate on a computer screen: see and feel how political boundaries interact with real terrain. Rivers, ridges, and valleys suddenly make more sense when the map is sitting on a table in front of you.

And since each county is removable, you can explore the state piece by piece.

The Auction Surprise

During the event, Ruekert & Mielke also purchased an additional small map that was donated to the silent auction. There was a lot more interest than I expected, and the bidding got surprisingly competitive.

For anyone who didn't manage to win it… I may have some good news coming soon.

What's Next?

This project was a blast to build, and I'm already thinking about ways to expand it. Future versions could include features like:

  • Highways and major roads
  • Water and lakes
  • County population density visualized with elevation
  • Other states or regions

For now, I'm just happy that I was able to take my question to a whole new level.

And apparently, people think it's pretty unique too.
