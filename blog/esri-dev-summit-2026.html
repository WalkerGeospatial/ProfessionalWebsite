7 Takeaways from the Esri Developer & Technology Summit 2026

Nathan Walker  ·  March 15, 2026  ·  4 min read

1. The food is always amazing.

Anything with mochi ice cream is an automatic 10/10.

2. Esri's integration of AI is honestly impressive.

Many companies shoehorn AI into their products. Esri's approach feels measured and thoughtful.

  • AI in tools like Arcade and Python Notebooks is minimally invasive from a UI/UX perspective. If you do not want to use it, you do not have to.
  • Organizations can easily disable it if needed.
  • The upcoming AI tools left me excited rather than skeptical.
  • The discussions around MCP servers and the examples Esri shared were excellent.
  • The transparency from Esri engineers about how they use AI internally was refreshing.
  • The session "AI-Infused Development: Lessons Learned and Patterns from Esri Engineers" was one of the best practical guides I have seen for using AI in development. Great work from Raul Jimenez Ortega.

3. Developers are clearly using AI heavily.

Tools like Claude Code and Codex are already changing how many developers approach writing, debugging, and prototyping code.

4. The Data Science and Geoprocessing teams consistently deliver great sessions.

Even at a conference focused primarily on developers, these remain some of my favorite talks to attend.

5. 3D felt slightly less prominent than last year.

My guess is that Esri's AI efforts took center stage this year as many of those tools are now coming to fruition. That said, there are still a lot of exciting developments happening in the 3D space.

6. There is a lot of potential in Esri's computer vision tools.

The object detection and segmentation models in Living Atlas and ArcGIS Pro continue to improve. With all the attention on generative AI, I think these capabilities are somewhat overlooked, but there is real potential there.

7. The GIS industry is entering an interesting transition period.

Much of the old guard in the field is approaching retirement, and a lot of existing technology is aging. That likely means a lot of opportunity for younger developers to step up and help shape the next generation of tools and systems.

Overall, it was a great conference with a lot to think about. Excited to see where things go over the next year, and I can't wait to go back!
